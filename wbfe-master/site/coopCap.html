Cooperative Capitalism

Why are corporate leaders raising alarms about the dangers of pursuing profit above all else? Why are Silicon Valley billionaires sequestering themselves in rural retreats to contemplate changing the very systems that made them rich? It is not their conscience or ethics that drive these concerns, it’s self-preservation. In a traditional capitalist system, we are taught that wealth and shareholder return equate to success. Unfortunately, those outside the profit loop often pay the price when corporations practice this “predatory capitalism” and grow without concern for their impact on society. The US Congress is contemplating antitrust action against some of these corporations and others will likely be next. This looming threat is likely what drove the CEOs of 192 of the world’s largest corporations to create a new Statement of Purpose for a Corporation as part of their Business Roundtable , committing to “support(ing) the communities in which we work. We respect the people in our communities and protect the environment by embracing sustainable practices across our businesses”. Of course, this commitment is voluntary and can be tossed aside when profits slide.

Failing to address community and employee needs is not only bad for a corporation’s image, it affects performance as well. A recent Baylor University study study shows that managers who put profit above all else fail to get the performance they seek from employees.

The White Buffalo Co-op practices Cooperative Capitalism, a system that fixes the problem of negative social impact and employee production in a simple, but effective way. All Co-op employees are Patrons who still have an opportunity for wealth, but will never face the threat of breakup or consumer pushback as a result of their damage to society. As a Community Development Entity, the Co-op must use 29% of profits for development programs in underserved, low-income communities. Instead of reserving the remaining profits for shareholders and founders, Co-op profits are shared by business leaders, staff, customers and the surrounding community of Co-op Patrons. Rather than taking advantage of communities by conducting business without concern for impact, Co-op businesses start by researching how a business might impact all stakeholders, whether they are customers or not. The Co-op actively works to bring those outside the profit loop into our Co-op profit share through credit program services and the opportunity to earn money, regardless of their current economic status or lack of assets.

Self-preservation is part of human nature and inspires the greed that helps predatory capitalists thrive. Cooperative Capitalism offers entrepreneurs and the communities they serve an opportunity to thrive with a clear conscience and sustainable businesses.
